Project: Predictive Sports Betting Model

Pro Table Tennis

1. The Problem & Goal

My thesis was that the most significant edges are found not in data-rich, efficient markets, but in niche arenas where information is scarce. I targeted professional table tennis—a less liquid market where I believed major inefficiencies were likely to exist. The goal was to engineer a professional-grade quantitative model to systematically discover features that correlate with player performance. The objective was not simply to predict a winner, but to identify alpha, build a strategy around it, and capitalize on value bets where the model's probabilities diverged significantly from the market's line.

2. Data & Feature Engineering

The market offers the same raw data to every participant; the edge, therefore, cannot come from the data itself, but from its interpretation. My advantage was built by engineering proprietary signals the general market overlooks. The critical task was to capture the true dynamics of a matchup—factors that simple win/loss records obscure. My script, `advanced_feature_engineering.py`, systematically constructed predictive variables from the ground up: momentum performance metrics, psychological factors (advanced head-to-head stats), and relative skill (ELO rating differentials). This converted a simple ledger of past games into an actionable, multi-dimensional analytics dataset.

3. Methodology & Model

The system was built using a Gradient Boosting Classifier, chosen for its performance in handling complex, non-linear relationships. The entire validation process was governed by a single, non-negotiable principle: "Point-in-Time" correctness. A rigorous simulation loop processed each match chronologically, re-calculating every predictive feature on-the-fly using only the historical data available at that exact moment. This eliminated all forms of lookahead bias and ensured every decision was a perfect replica of a real-world scenario.

This simulation generated a high-fidelity log of the model's raw predictions. A critical insight was uncovered during forensic analysis of this log, which revealed a definitive signal in the noise—the core market inefficiency the model was built to find.

This discovery was translated directly into a hard-coded, non-discretionary filter for the final strategy. A definitive back-test, with this filter enabled, validated the system's historical profitability and confirmed that a larger perceived edge correlated directly with a higher ROI. The model's final output is a true probability; when it diverges significantly from the market's line, it signals an actionable, value-driven opportunity.

4. Results & Validation

A comprehensive, point-in-time correct backtest against multiple seasons of historical data validated the model's edge. The system demonstrated a statistically significant, positive return on investment. Below are the key performance metrics from the final backtest, which simulated 3,522 trades.

The objective is to collect a statistically significant sample of real-money trades to validate the backtested performance.

About Me

My education in markets began not with theory, but with practice—first in elementary arbitrage, and later in building predictive models using machine learning. I have always been a student of the game, whatever game I was playing. This path taught me a singular lesson: that any system, be it a market or a machine, has inefficiencies. My entire professional focus has been dedicated to identifying and capitalizing on them.

For nearly a decade, my classroom was the professional poker table. It was a masterclass not in cards, but in applied probability, risk management, and emotional discipline. The market, in any form, is just a larger table with more players, and the principles remain the same.

Now, that entire education has come full circle. The engineer's rigor, the investor's patience, and the poker player's probablistic thinking have all converged on building predictive models.
